  • A MAC (Media Access Control) address is a unique identifier assigned to a network interface controller (NIC) for communication on a network. Think of it like a fingerprint for your device's network connection. Every device that can connect to a network, like your computer, smartphone, or router, has its own MAC address.
  • MAC addresses are typically assigned by the manufacturer of the network interface and are hardcoded into the device's hardware. They are used to ensure that data packets are sent to the correct device on a local network. Unlike IP addresses, which can change or be assigned dynamically, MAC addresses are permanent and globally unique.
  • MAC addresses are structured as a series of hexadecimal digits (0-9 and A-F), usually formatted in pairs separated by colons or dashes. For example, a MAC address might look like this: 00:1A:2B:3C:4D:5E.
  • While MAC addresses are essential for network communication, they can also be used for tracking and identification purposes. As such, some users may take steps to protect their privacy by limiting the sharing of their MAC address whenever possible.
